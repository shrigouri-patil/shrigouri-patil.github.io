Shrigouri Patil

I am a research enthusiast interested in learning new things every day. Over the last five years, I have gained extensive experience in protein and molecular biology, covering diverse areas such as molecular cloning, protein studies, gene editing, and the development of diagnostic kits. As I continue to learn, I will update this webpage to share what I have discovered.

My scientific journey began with a small project on producing biofuel from coconut shells. For this project, my teammates and I searched for the best microbe in the local area to help break down complex fibers in coconut shells into simple ethanol. Cultivating these fungi and exploring their capabilities was an exciting start to my passion for science.

This newfound interest brought me to the Vellore Institute of Technology, where I pursued a Master’s degree in Biomedical Genetics. During my first semester, I had the opportunity to handle mammalian cells for the first time. My project focused on exploring the reasons behind neurodegeneration and how toxin accumulation over time might increase neurodegenerative disorders. Our work culminated in a paper presentation that won first prize at the end of the semester.

Another notable project during my Master’s studies involved understanding the effects of nanoparticles on the environment. I cultured primary cells such as red blood cells (RBCs) and lymphocytes to assess the impact of polystyrene nanoparticles on human cells. This work not only helped me develop expertise in primary cell culture but also led to the publication of my first research paper in the Journal of Environmental Science and Pollution Research, where we concluded that higher nanoparticle concentrations are harmful to human blood cells.

For my Master’s thesis and subsequent Junior Research Fellow (JRF) role, I joined Prof. Gaiti Hasan's lab, where the focus was on understanding neuronal networks and the importance of calcium signaling using Drosophila melanogaster. My thesis investigated how balanced nutrition during development affects long-term health and behavior. Observing flies and brainstorming innovative ways to measure developmental defects was both challenging and rewarding. I extended this research during my JRF role, analyzing molecular mechanisms through experiments like qRT-PCR on neuropeptides and neuropeptide receptors. Under Dr. Megha’s mentorship, I not only completed my thesis but also presented my findings at the Indian Society of Developmental Biology (InSDB) conference in 2018. This work was later included in a paper currently under preprint.

Following this, I joined the Tata Institute of Genetics and Society (TIGS) as a Research Assistant under Prof. Baskar Bakthavachalu. My project focused on understanding the molecular mechanisms of insecticide resistance in mosquitoes, specifically the role of voltage-gated sodium channels. I designed and executed experiments to clone and express this challenging 6kb gene using techniques such as Gibson assembly, overlap extension PCR, and yeast-based cloning. After several iterations, I successfully expressed the protein and optimized its purification for Cryo-EM structural studies, gaining critical expertise in protein expression and purification over three years.

Building on my molecular biology expertise, I transitioned to gene editing as a Facility Assistant at the Fly Facility at the National Center for Biological Sciences. Here, I led the molecular team, strategizing and executing genome editing projects using CRISPR-Cas9 and PiggyBac. I also established efficient workflows for creating and screening mutations in transgenic flies. Dr. Deepti Trivedi guided me through these projects, which provided me with hands-on experience in advanced gene editing.

Most recently, I worked as a Research Scientist at CRISPRBITS under the mentorship of Dr. Reety Arora, who has been an exceptional guide and inspiration. At CRISPRBITS, I developed a point-of-care diagnostic kit to detect antimicrobial resistance using CRISPR technology. This role gave me a firsthand understanding of translational research and the challenges of reproducibility in diagnostic assays. Collaborating with clinicians and microbiologists, I independently developed an assay and authored my first paper as the lead author, describing the protocol we established.

Now, after five years of diverse research experience, I am taking a break to learn bioinformatics through open-source courses. My goal is to combine my biological expertise with computational tools to support cutting-edge research. I will document my learning journey and small projects on this website.
